Five things that break between your model and your spec book

Your set goes out the door with the assumption that every keynote on every sheet maps to a paragraph in the project manual. That assumption is wrong on most projects. The breaks are usually small — until one of them shows up as a change order.

01

Orphaned keynotes

A keynote tag references a spec section that doesn't exist in the project manual. Most common cause: the spec was authored from a template that didn't include the relevant section, and nobody updated it.

02

Sub-section truncation

A keynote points to 08 11 13.16 — a specific door type — but only the parent section 08 11 13 was written. The match looks clean at the division level. The actual sub-section isn't resolved.

03

Spec sections in scope but never called out

The reverse of orphaned. The spec book has a section the project will need but no keynote in the model references it. Either the section is dead scope or the drawings missed an annotation.

04

Mismatched scope

The keynote on the drawing reads "Steel Door" and the spec section's first paragraph reads "Aluminum-Framed Storefront." Both legitimate. Neither matches the other. This is the kind of thing that becomes deposition material when there's a claim.

05

Custom-scheme entries with no spec mapping

Your firm uses custom alphanumeric keynotes — D08, ARCH-12. Some of them carry a CSI section reference in the description; others don't. The ones that don't are silent coordination orphans.

+

And the way you're checking today

Most firms run this audit by hand. Project architect or junior staff opens the spec book, opens the model, walks through tag by tag. On a 1,000-keynote project this takes hours. Humans miss things — that's not a character flaw, it's a fact about repetitive cross-referencing.

What Proof actually does

No AI guessing. No semantic interpretation. No "trust us." Three deterministic steps, every flag in the report answers why with specific evidence.

01

Reads your model

Scans every keynote tag in the view you have open — or every sheet in the project at once. Catches all three Revit keynote sources: Element, Material, and User-typed. Also reads your firm's custom alphanumeric schemes — not just CSI MasterFormat.

02

Parses your spec manual

Single PDF or folder of one-PDF-per-section spec files (the format SpecLink, BSD, and Deltek produce). Filename-as-truth heuristic for folder mode. Skips and counts scanned pages without crashing the run.

03

Produces a verifiable report

Every keynote tag matched, orphaned, or division-flagged. Sortable, filterable, click-to-locate-element-in-Revit, mark-as-resolved with persistent state. Export to CSV for your QA records.

Who Proof is for — and who it isn't

Honest positioning saves everyone time. If your firm doesn't fit the column on the left, Proof won't help you on this set — and a demo isn't going to change that. We'd rather you know now than in week three of an evaluation.

Built for

  • Architects, BIM coordinators, and project managers using Revit 2026
  • Firms that use Revit's keynote system — Element, Material, or User-source on Keynote Tag elements
  • Projects with a written spec manual (CSI MasterFormat or a consistent custom scheme)
  • Firms that want to verify coordination at the milestone gates — SD, DD, CD — not after issuance
  • Practices that maintain firm-level keynote standards (or want to start)

Not built for

  • Drawing sets that don't reference a spec manual
  • Firms that use only TextNote-with-leader annotations and never adopted Revit's keynote system
  • Pre-Revit-2026 versions (Revit 2025 / 2024 support possible later — not today)
  • AutoCAD-only or Vectorworks-only practices (Revit add-in)
  • Anyone wanting AI-generated spec interpretation — Proof is deterministic by design, not generative
